My Diary
Introduction
A Vision for a Transformed Nation

My aspiration, my dream, has always been to transform our developing nation into a developed one—a land where its people embody the true essence of Indian culture, free from dogma, embracing all faiths in harmony. This vision is not mine alone; it resonates with anyone who has ever dared to dream of such a future and continues to do so. However, a developed nation is not merely one rich in material wealth or cultural heritage. It is a nation whose people possess the ability to address all contemporary challenges, whether those of today, a century ago, or a century hence.

In a truly developed nation, leaders lead with integrity, actors perform with authenticity, saints deliver sermons with sincerity, scientists innovate with purpose, and every worker performs their duties naturally, as an extension of their being. Even the pursuit of liberation (moksha) is a form of action, driven by a desire that, at its core, is self-serving, shaped by the level of knowledge one possesses. While these actions may lead to material progress for individuals and the nation, they alone cannot elevate its cultural or spiritual stature, nor can they establish lasting peace.

The hallmark of a developed nation lies in its distinct qualities: leaders who govern without the need for elections, actors whose stories captivate without superficiality, saints who share wisdom without rivalry, scientists who seek truth beyond mere invention, and citizens who harbor no grievances against the work of others. Without these qualities, even a so-called developed nation remains, in essence, a developing one.

Five groups of people shape a nation’s rise or fall:

  • Saints and spiritual guides
  • Scientists and innovators
  • Leaders and policymakers
  • Artists and performers
  • The common people, whose thoughts often diverge from the above groups.

A nation’s ascent cannot be achieved through mere speeches, sermons, or books. It requires a clear policy, supported by sub-policies, rooted in sacrifice—not of blood, but of wealth and fleeting pleasures—and unity among political factions, artists, and spiritual leaders. This vision can be realized in two ways:

  1. Through Collective Responsibility
    The nation’s leaders and citizens, present or future, must uphold responsibility, sincerity, and honesty to implement these policies systematically.
  2. The Cycle of Time (Kaalchakra)
    A universal truth encompassing natural and unnatural events—wars, cosmic phenomena, or predictions by seers, astrologers, and scientists. Observing the current state of our nation and the turning of time’s wheel, it may take another century for our country to truly become developed. Life is like a game where a single player may lead or lag, whether the game is crafted by humans or by the divine.

Yet, the paradox is striking: a nation will indeed become developed, first through the disciplined execution of policies (Method I), but its ultimate realization will stem from a profound shift in human consciousness (Method II), not from cataclysmic destruction.

Many enlightened souls have been born to guide the nation toward this goal, and many more will come. Yet, few achieve complete success on their own terms. Why? Because of two reasons:

  1. The divine order of the universe.
  2. Flawed approaches.

Some were martyred, some embraced asceticism, some attained sainthood, and others left a lasting legacy before departing. Such individuals are not born by chance; their wisdom, shaped by the consciousness of past lives, enables them to bring transformative change. This wisdom is not bestowed directly but is earned through the trials and triumphs of life’s circumstances, freeing them from worldly attachments.

What is the meaning of life? What is its purpose? Where is God? What is divine play? What is creation—its nature, purpose, duration? What is time? What is infinity—its essence, purpose, duration? And ultimately, who am I? These are eternal questions that shape a person’s character and philosophy.

A Scenario: You need to fill your vehicle with fuel, but you have no money.

A Circumstance: You have money, but the fuel station has no fuel. Your circumstance mirrors the station’s situation.

Philosophy (Tattvagyan): The knowledge to discern the unseen essence of these questions through one’s inner self, independent of external teachings or books, derived solely from life’s situations and circumstances.

Science: The knowledge to understand the tangible aspects of these questions through observation and reasoning.

Spiritual Wisdom: The subtle understanding of these questions, leading to enlightenment. A yogi who attains this becomes a knower of the ultimate truth, often choosing samadhi—a conscious departure from life. While ordinary suicide stems from despair and ignorance, samadhi is a celebrated, positive transcendence. In samadhi, thought ceases, and the infinite curve of consciousness merges into the eternal.

Why were great souls more abundant a century or two ago, yet so rare today? Spiritual progress can be intentional (with resolve, involving sacrifice) or natural (inclusive, moving with others). Intentional progress brings visible change; natural progress brings subtle transformation.

Knowledge = Science [Finite] + Philosophy (Infinite) + Spiritual Wisdom [Finite]

Soul: The invisible force animating all living beings.

Consciousness: The principles of life, rooted in the depth and truth of thoughts.

Mind: The seat of desires, born from consciousness, giving rise to needs and actions.

General vs. Specific Knowledge

Aspect General Knowledge Specific Knowledge
Nature Expressible, like counting to a finite number—words. Inexpressible, like counting to infinity—imagination. Thoughts lie between, either spoken or concealed.
Clarity Easily understood, like recording a song or recalling past experiences. Initially confusing but gradually clear, aided by future possibilities, stories, or films.
Source Books, past experiences, conversations with others. Nature, the mind, and the soul; an inner dialogue within one’s consciousness.
Process Discontinuous, forming a finite circle with a defined diameter, like a closed thought loop. Continuous, forming an infinite circle with an undefined diameter—an infinite expanse of thought.

There, they regain their past spiritual wisdom effortlessly and strive harder for divine realization, drawn irresistibly toward God, transcending worldly desires.

SrimadBhagavadGeeta, Chapter 10, Verses 10–11: To those devoted to me with love, I grant the wisdom to attain me. Out of compassion, I dwell in their hearts, dispelling the darkness of ignorance with the light of truth.

Everything on Earth—natural or man-made, living or inanimate—is governed by divine programming. Yet, God, the programmer, does not interfere with this design. Thus, we reap what we sow.

True liberation from worldly attachments is not exclusive to saints. Anyone free from the desire for rewards or attachment to outcomes, from childhood to youth, is worthy of greatness. Success is not measured by wealth or status. If corruption persists, if material progress overshadows cultural decline, the responsibility lies with the “special” individuals—those leading the flock—not the common people following behind.

In such times, amassing wealth or attaining sainthood does not equate to success if one fails to discern truth from falsehood. Many, swayed by early life’s challenges, abandon their duties, chasing wealth, status, or liberation, believing a Gandhi, a Narendra, a revolutionary saint, or even Lord Krishna will resolve all crises. Such self-centered resolve may bring personal prosperity, but it fails the nation. Why does righteousness falter repeatedly? Why must divine incarnations return time and again? Because God urges us to take responsibility, to stop relying on divine intervention. Yet, many misdirect their resolve toward personal gain rather than understanding the divine.

Transformation is inevitable, but a motor built for one floor cannot pump water to the tenth. Only a motor designed for higher capacity can serve all levels. Similarly, a single glass of water quenches one’s thirst but cannot satisfy a multitude. The greater one’s knowledge—scientific, philosophical, and spiritual—the higher their contribution. Yet, this knowledge must serve all, not just oneself.

In an ideal state, thoughts and thinking capacities align, not in uniformity of roles but in shared principles, allowing each to fulfill their duties with integrity. This harmony, driven by growing consciousness and knowledge, shaped by life’s circumstances and birth, is the path to peace. Science and philosophy relate like quotient and remainder in division—science is the tangible wonder left on Earth, while philosophy accompanies the soul, returning with new thoughts in each life.

Life does not merely repeat with new ideas; it presents its truths in ever-new ways, like stars shining eternally, whether we play the game or not.
